# Cold Call / Warm Call Scripts

*Use sparingly and legally. For most EU targets, email + LinkedIn first; call only warm leads, inbound, or where you have a legitimate basis and the contact expects business outreach. Calling is best for booked-but-no-show recovery and pilot follow-ups.*

> **Legal note:** unsolicited B2B calls are regulated (ePrivacy + national rules; some countries require prior consent or screening against opt-out registries). Prefer calling people who engaged with content, replied to email, or are existing trial users. When in doubt, don't cold-call — send a LinkedIn voice note instead.

---

## Opener (warm — they downloaded/snapshotted/replied)
> "Hi {First}, it's Ajay from Verispect — you {ran a snapshot / downloaded the AI Act guide / replied to my note} the other day. Caught you at an okay moment for 30 seconds?"
> *(yes)* → "Quick reason I called: your snapshot flagged {category} drift, and that's the kind of thing that bites in a security review. Worth 15 minutes to show you what continuous monitoring would catch? I can also generate your full report."

## Opener (permission-based cold, well-targeted)
> "Hi {First}, Ajay, founder of Verispect — I'll be quick and you can tell me to get lost. I work with {domain} teams running LLMs in {use case} on proving the model isn't drifting or biased for the EU AI Act. Is that something on your plate, or am I early?"
> *(pattern interrupt: ask permission, name the exact pain, give an easy out.)*

## Value statement (15 sec)
> "Most teams log what their model said but have no way to know it started behaving differently — until a buyer or regulator asks. We catch that automatically, one line of code, and we never see your data. Output is the audit PDF they ask for."

## Qualifying questions (pick 2–3)
- "Are LLMs making real decisions in your product — hiring, credit, that kind of thing?"
- "How would you know today if the model's behaviour shifted?"
- "Anything forcing the issue — an audit, an enterprise deal, the 2026 deadline?"

## The ask (book the meeting — that's the only goal of the call)
> "I don't want to pitch on the phone. Let me show it live for 15 minutes — I'll run it against your actual model so you see real numbers. Does {Tuesday 3pm or Wednesday 11am} work?"

## Common live objections (short verbal forms)
- "Busy" → "Totally — that's why I'm asking for 15 minutes, not now. Tuesday or Wednesday?"
- "Send an email" → "Will do — and I'll include a free snapshot of your own model so it's not generic. What's the best address? And can I pencil 15 min in case it's useful?"
- "We use Helicone" → "Keep it — we're the assurance layer on top, we don't replace logging. 15 min to show the difference?"
- "Not interested" → "Fair enough — mind if I ask, is it not a priority, or not a fit? Helps me not bug the wrong people." *(learn, then exit politely.)*

## Voicemail (≤20 sec)
> "Hi {First}, Ajay from Verispect — we help {domain} teams prove their LLMs aren't drifting or biased for the EU AI Act, one line of code, we never see your data. I'll send a quick email with a free snapshot option. If it's relevant, reply and we'll grab 15 minutes. Thanks {First}."

## LinkedIn voice note (often better than a call in the EU)
30–45 sec, same structure as the warm opener, ends with "no pressure — reply here if it's worth 15 minutes." Personal, async, lower legal risk, high response.

## After the call (always)
- Log outcome + next step + date.
- Send promised email within the hour (snapshot setup + sample PDF).
- Add to suppression list immediately if they decline contact.

## Mindset
The call's only job is to earn a 15-minute demo. Don't sell the product on the phone — sell the meeting. Be the most respectful, least pushy caller they get all week; for a trust brand, *how* you sell is part of the product.
